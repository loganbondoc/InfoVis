The Hidden Dangers of Fitness Content on Social Media

Fitness and health are an integral part of any balanced lifestyle. But you know those people, the gym bros, the fitness freaks. "How many calories are in this?" "I need to hit my protein intake." To outward appearance it may seem like overwhelming passion for nutritious living, but whatâ€™s really going on under the surface?

Fitness influencers are powerhouses on social media platforms, with hashtags such as #fitness amassing 510 million followers and #fitnessmotivation having 135 million followers on Instagram in 2024. Not only that, their endorsement for a plethora of health and fitness products, contributes to a market that has exploded from 1.7 billion USD in 2016 to a projected 24 billion in 2024. Their impact is undeniable, yet a critical question looms large:

Are these influencers trustworthy sources
of health and fitness advice?

A recent examination into the content disseminated by fitness personalities reveals a troubling lack of credibility. The University of Glasgow conducted a study evaluating health food products endorsed by the top 9 health food blog influencers in 2018. Alarmingly, 8 out of 9 of these influencers failed the assessment on a number of criteria, while every single one missed their calorie targets.

In a study conducted by BMC Public Health, 100 of the top Instagram accounts using the hashtag #fitspiration were evaluated for the credibility of their workout information and advice provided through video content. The findings highlighted that a significant number of these accounts were not credible sources of fitness advice as their content had a strong focus on appearance and objectification of the body, as opposed to health and functionality which leads to a rise in body image concern and eating disorders (Prichard et al., 2008).

Moreover, a correlation exists between the time spent on social media and self-image related behavior. In a study conducted by Souvik, users were asked to rank their feelings towards statements on a scale of 1-5. The findings convey that the longer time individuals spend on these platforms, there is a heightened need for validation-seeking behavior as users draw self-deprecating comparisons between themselves and the success of others.

Research by Dr B. Jiotsa points to two primary theories that explain how social media can negatively impact body image.

Comparison theory suggests that individuals who frequently compare themselves to others deemed more attractive or fit are prone to body dissatisfaction, a process intensified by social media exposure.

Objectification theory highlights the impact of societal norms, where women and girls are socialized to view their bodies from an external perspective, leading to self-objectification and body dissatisfaction. Similar patterns are observed in men and boys, with media promoting ideals of leanness and muscularity.

This creates a vicious cycle where the lack of credibility and qualifications among fitness influencers leads to the spread of inaccurate and misleading health advice. The health food products they promote often fail to deliver the promised results, leaving people dissatisfied with their bodies. The oversaturation of such content adversely impacts users' mental wellbeing, fostering validation-seeking behavior and body dysmorphia. As a result, individuals are more inclined to purchase these ineffective products and continue consuming misleading content, perpetuating the cycle of dissatisfaction and misinformation.

But what can we do about it?

Reach out

Seeking help from a loved ones or a mental health professional can provide invaluable support for those struggling with body dysmorphia. If you or anyone you know is struggling, there are plenty of support services to reach out to:
The Butterfly National Helpline: 1800 33 4673
Kids Helpline: 1800 551 800
BeyondBlue: 1300 22 4636

Do your research

Before purchasing health and fitness products, take the time to investigate their claims and the qualifications of those endorsing them. Look for evidence-based reviews and consult credible sources to ensure the products are both safe and effective.

Take a break from social media

Reducing your exposure to social media can help alleviate the constant comparison and pressure to meet unrealistic body standards. Taking regular breaks or limiting screen time can significantly improve your mental well-being and self-image.

Change your habits

Adopting healthier habits can make a significant difference. Gradually facing anxiety-provoking situations without resorting to rituals and engaging in healthier mirror-related behaviors can help challenge negative beliefs and reduce body dysmorphia.
